Student Frequently Asked Questions


If I do not sign the pledge or word it correctly on an assignment, am I safe from an Honor System violation?

The Honor System requires that undergraduate students sign the pledge on all submitted work (tests, quizzes, projects, code, etc.). If the pledge is not initially present on an assignment, the course instructor will take the omission as an unintentional mistake and ask that you submit the signed pledge. If you refuse to sign the pledge at this stage, it will be taken as an admission of responsibility for an Honor System violation, and the professor can report the case to the Honor Board.

Is it acceptable to use a stamp of the pledge in lieu of writing it out?

Yes, a stamp or a typed version of the pledge is acceptable in place of a handwritten pledge. A valid signature must be included. For a typed assignment, typing one’s name is an acceptable alternative to a handwritten signature.

Should tutors or classmates be listed as references on homework and assignments?

Yes. When the professor allows collaboration, even working with classmates counts as aid and must be referenced on the assignment.

My professor has accused me of cheating, and decided to fail me or give me a zero on the assignment. What should I do?

Report the situation to the Honor Board. The incident will be investigated by the Honor Board. Professors may only investigate potential violations through the Faculty Adjudication process.

What is a Faculty Adjudication? How does the process work?

If a professor suspects that you may have committed an Honor System violation, the professor may approach you about their suspicions and discuss the matter directly with you. You and your professor may mutually come to a decision on a penalty, and the faculty member must submit a completed Faculty Adjudication Report Form found here to the Honor Board. The Honor Board will then vote to approve the Faculty Adjudication and the student and professor will receive a Case Outcome Notification to indicate the closure of the incident. Please note that the Honor Board reserves the right to open a full investigation on behalf of the student if it believes that there was improper conduct throughout the process. Additionally, the Honor Board may modify the agreed-upon penalty if it does not fall within the established Penalty Matrix found here.

My professor approached me regarding a faculty adjudication, but I didn’t do anything wrong or the penalty is too harsh. Can the Honor Board investigate?

Yes. As long as you did not sign agreeing to the penalty, you can report your case to the Honor Board, at which point it is out of the professor’s hands.If you did sign the Faculty Adjudication Report Form, please contact the Honor Board and the case may still be reopened in certain circumstances, determined on a case-by-case basis.

I think I saw my classmate cheating during an exam, but I’m not sure. Should I report it, and if so, how?

Yes, even if you’re not sure, it’s your responsibility to report anything you see. The Honor Board will investigate further and determine if there is evidence of a violation. You can report all violations on the Honor System website by clicking “Report a Violation” or by clicking “Report a Concern” on your MyStevens homepage.

I am being investigated for an Honor System violation. Am I allowed to consult with my friend who is on the Honor Board?

Any case details will be restricted to meetings with the investigative committee (ICC and ICA) assigned to the case, who have no personal ties to involved students to avoid a conflict of interest. However, a Procedural Advisor (PA) will be assigned to you to answer any questions you may have about how the Honor System operates. You may also consult the Honor Board’s Advisors, Dean Berkley (dberkley@stevens.edu), Dr. Linda Beninghove (lbeningh@stevens.edu), or Director Xhijola Ruçi (xruci@stevens.edu).

As an undergraduate student, does the Honor System apply in my graduate courses?

Yes. Undergraduates enrolled in 500-level courses are subject to the Honor System in exactly the same way as 400-level and lower courses. Cases function in the same manner, with an investigative committee, procedural advisor, and potential case outcomes of a case drop, confession, stipulated confession, and hearing. Students in 600-level and higher courses are considered graduate students in that course, and therefore are subject to the Graduate Code of Academic Integrity.

What is an Academic Sanction? What is a Removable Academic Sanction, and how can it be removed?

An Academic Sanction is a mark on a student’s transcript next to a course indicating that the student was found responsible of an Honor System violation in that course. An Academic Sanction is removed upon a student’s graduation from Stevens or departure from the University. If the Honor Board assigns a Removable Academic Sanction, the transcript mark can be removed prior to graduation by completing a task, which is typically a version of the Ethics Course (the specifics are detailed in a student’s individual Case Outcome Notification). If a student completes their assigned task in the period of time allotted on a Case Outcome Notification, the Honor Board will inform the Honor Board Advisor to confer with the Office of the Registrar to remove the Academic Sanction, a process which is typically completed within 1 (one) week of the request.

After an Academic Sanction has been removed, is there any trace that I had it?

No. A student’s transcript and official records will not indicate an Honor System violation. If an employer, graduate school, or other entity requests records relating to academic misconduct from Stevens, the student will first be contacted to request permission for the Honor Board to release this information. If the student permits the release of the information, an Honor Board Advisor will contact the Honor Board for a student’s case history and will provide that information to the requesting organization or entity.

What is an Ethics Course?

The Ethics Course is an Honor Board-designed independent study course that is meant to serve as a way for a student found responsible for an Honor System violation to reflect on the incident and the importance of academic integrity. The Ethics Course includes four Tiers assigned based upon the severity of the incident, and other mitigating and aggravating factors. Each Tier includes a set of tasks which must be completed within a specified time frame. Ethics Courses are most often used as a method to remove a Removable Academic Sanction (as described above).

Can I appeal the results of an Honor Board investigation after I took responsibility or was found responsible of a violation?

Yes. You may appeal the outcome of an investigation to the Committee on Academic Appeals if you believe the Honor Board did not properly follow procedure or the assigned penalty was too harsh. If you signed a Stipulated Confession Form, you may not appeal on the grounds of the penalty’s harshness. In order to initiate an appeal, please follow the instructions listed in your Case Outcome Notification.

What are some examples of penalties levied by the Honor Board?

Honor Board penalties typically fall within three categories: grade changes, Academic Sanctions, and suspensions or expulsions. Grade changes typically range from partial credit on an assignment to failure in a course. Academic Sanctions are described in detail above. Suspensions typically range from one-semester to one-year, based upon the individual case. For consistency, the Honor Board references its established Penalty Matrix (found here) whenever voting on a penalty. Assigned penalties typically fall within the range of the Matrix, however case-specific circumstances may cause an assigned penalty to fall outside of the recommended range.

Should I take responsibility for a violation if I know I’m responsible?

Yes. The student-run Honor System relies on students holding themselves and others accountable. Being honest about an incident is extremely helpful to Honor Board investigators.

Should I take responsibility for the violation if I know I'm not responsible?

No. Only ever admit responsibility for a violation if you actually committed the violation. The Honor System’s procedures are in place in order to limit situations in which students are found responsible for violations they did not commit. Being honest about an incident is extremely helpful to Honor Board investigators.

What are potential case outcomes for Honor System investigations?

An Honor Board investigation has four potential outcomes: case drop, confession, stipulated confession, and hearing. A case drop occurs when the Honor Board feels there is not substantive evidence to pursue the case further and votes to drop the case at a General Body Meeting. A confession occurs when a student admits responsibility for a violation. The Honor Board votes on a penalty based on established precedent and the Penalty Matrix and confers the assigned penalty to the student and professor via a Case Outcome Notification. A stipulated confession is a confession form with a pre-determined penalty voted upon by the Honor Board listed. This enables a student to know the penalty that will be assigned to them prior to admitting responsibility. A student’s requesting of a stipulated confession does not imply responsibility. Upon signing the Stipulated Confession Form, a student is admitting responsibility for an Honor System violation and will receive a Case Outcome Notification with the penalty that was listed on the Form. Finally, a hearing results when a student does not admit responsibility for a violation, and the Honor Board feels there is substantive evidence to pursue the case. Hearings are explained below.

What is an Honor System hearing?

If a student does not admit responsibility for an Honor System violation, and the Honor Board feels there is enough evidence to pursue a case, a hearing will be scheduled. Prior to the hearing, a randomized jury call will be sent to members of the Stevens undergraduate community calling for their participation in the hearing proceedings. Of these students, eight will be selected to hear a case, in which both the accused student and the Honor Board investigators will have the opportunity to speak to the evidence collected throughout an investigation. Six of the eight jurors will then enter deliberation and render a unanimous verdict of Responsible or Not Responsible for the Honor System violation.

If I witness a violation, how long do I have to report it?

Students, faculty, teaching assistants, and other community members can report an Honor System violation within fifteen business days of the suspected violation’s occurrence. Any reports made more than fifteen business days after an incident occurred may only be investigated by a three-fourths majority of the Honor Board at its next General Body Meeting.
