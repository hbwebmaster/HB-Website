Guide for the Potential Jury Member


Introduction

This page serves as general outline of pertinent information that applies to being a potential jury member for an Honor System Hearing.

Your Role in the Stevens Honor System

Serving as a jury member in an Honor System Hearing is an extremely important part of the Stevens Honor System. The unique student-run system we have in place here at Stevens wouldn't be possible without participation in jury duty by the Stevens undergraduate community. A jury member has a very important job in deciding whether or not a student is responsible of the suspected violation, based on the evidence presented to you. The Honor Board never determines responsibility in Honor System investigations. Thus, if an accused student decides not to confess to a violation, it is up to the jury to look at the evidence and decide which is more likely and deliver a verdict. So, thank you for doing your part in upholding the Honor System and maintaining academic integrity here at Stevens.

From Potential Juror To Juror

Upon arriving at the Hearing Location, each potential jury member will be asked to sit while an Honor Board member marks your attendance. After attending a brief jury member training, the Judge of the Hearing will name the eight jury members. After this announcement, all others will be excused and the eight jury members will remain for the duration of the Presentation of Evidence and following Deliberation.

Preponderance of Evidence

The way in which Honor System Hearings are decided is based on preponderance of the evidence. This means to view the evidence presented in terms of quality, not quantity, and decide whther or not the student likely committed the violation or not. The goal is NOT to prove beyond a resonable doubt, but to instead determine which is more likely. As a jury member, it is important to make this distinction when reviewing the evidence.

The Presentation of Evidence

The Presentation of Evidence is when the jury has been selected and both the Investigative Team and the Accused Student will get to present their evidence in the Honor System investigation. They may display case-related documents, describe their side of the case, or question witnesses. As a jury member, you may not interrupt with questions. Any and all questions should be saved until after the Presentation of Evidence to be asked during Deliberation. You will receive an Evidence Packet with all case documents to follow along. It is recommended to write any notes or questions down on this packet.

Deliberation

The Deliberation phase of a hearing is when a jury member actively does their part for the Honor System. Behind closed doors, the jury members will review their evidence packets and Jury Instruction Guide and must come to a unanimous decision on whether or not it is likely the student is responsible for the suspected violation. At this time, questions may be asked in the form of notes delivered from the deliberation room to the Investigative Team and Accused Student, who agree on an answer and send it back. Consider both the objective evidence in the packet and the quality of any witness's testimony. Ultimately, it is up to the jury members to determine preponderance of the evidence and deliver a verdict.

Inability To Attend Hearing

If a potential jury member is unable to attend for the duration of the hearing (Approximately 1 - 3 hours), they must indicate it in the form linked in the initial jury duty call email.
